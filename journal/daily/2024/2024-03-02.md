# Daily Journal — Saturday, 2024-03-02

## Journal
It’s my first official day of medical leave and I don’t really know how to feel about the fact that it’s a whole six months of time to figure out my life essentially.

I realized last night that this is the first time in my whole life that I’ve had this much time off anything. At most, I’ve had 2–3 months off during summer breaks. When I finished school, I was employed after 2 months and haven’t had more than 2 weeks off in the last 6–7 years. That’s pretty nuts.

I’ve had this feeling of wanting to go with the momentum and get everything done that I’ve been putting off, just to get it out of the way. It’s been 3 months since December—about half the time of my official leave—and time went by fast. A few things have changed, but mostly, everything is the same.

The difference now is that I’ll have 100% of my time just for myself. No distractions, no excuse of being “at work” or “busy” or “stressed,” with no energy left for something else. I’m worried that I’ll realize, after all this time, something I already know deep down: that I don’t want to go back. Then comes the anxiety of what I’ll do after… how I’ll earn money.

The bigger dilemma is: what do I actually want? Part of me wants ambition, success, accomplishment in my career. Another part just wants to live life, to enjoy it without thinking everything is a sacrifice for something bigger in the future.

What am I sacrificing for? Money? Something in my gut says it’s secondary. Comfort? The discomfort of sacrifice is too high a price to pay. And I’m realizing that “price” is now synonymous with my well-being. Health is wealth. Or maybe more importantly, wealth is health. What good is money if the payoff is my physical, mental, and emotional health?

Maybe I say this now because I’m in a more comfortable financial situation, with medical leave still covering bills. But then my mind wanders—what if I didn’t have a job? What if I had resigned? Then all the lab tests and costs would come out of my savings. There’s no such thing as free lunch.

It’s the classic chicken-or-egg question: would my health have gotten this bad if I had a different job and lifestyle? Would I have been able to afford 6 months off—3 months of them paid—if I was somewhere else?

By September, I’ll have no more leaves left for the year, since all of them are being used now. That fact feels small compared to the possibility—strong one—that I’ll want to resign. Everyone is expecting it. Either I don’t return, or I return only to hand in my resignation.

Chichi gaslit me into believing that, but I also want to prove everyone wrong. I will return. And I will leave a positive mark: that I did what I said I would do, that I tried to make it work as much as I could.

And if I realize there’s something different I want to do, I’ll leave gracefully, respectfully, decently.

While I’m still there, the question remains: do I want to be ambitious? Yes. I’ll give my all and fight until the end to show them how good I am. I’ll save, rebuild my money, and move on—doing something I enjoy, or learning and growing somewhere else.
